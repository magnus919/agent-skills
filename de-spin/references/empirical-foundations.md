# Empirical Foundations and Boundaries

This skill is designed around evidence-backed reasoning practices. These sources support the method; they do not make the skill a universal truth machine.

## Lateral reading over surface credibility

Wineburg and McGrew observed historians, undergraduates, and professional fact-checkers evaluating live websites. Fact-checkers left sites quickly to investigate them through independent sources, reaching more warranted conclusions than participants who evaluated sites from their internal design, logos, credentials, and About pages.

- Source: [Wineburg & McGrew, *Lateral Reading*](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3048994)
- Use in this skill: trace provenance externally before trusting presentation.
- Limitation: observational comparison of 45 participants; it describes expert practice, not a guarantee that every search succeeds.

## Literal truth can still mislead

FTC guidance treats both express and implied claims as relevant and evaluates an advertisement’s net impression. It notes that testimonials are not substantiation, and that a claim broader than its supporting evidence is not adequately substantiated.

- Sources: [FTC Truth in Advertising](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/topics/truth-advertising), [FTC Myths and Half-Truths](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/speeches/myths-half-truths-about-deceptive-advertising)
- Use in this skill: separate literal wording, likely implication, scope, and typical result.
- Limitation: US consumer-protection guidance; it supplies a strong reasoning model, not a universal legal test.

## Volume and repetition are not corroboration

RAND’s analysis of the “firehose of falsehood” identifies high-volume, multichannel, rapid, repetitive messaging, with little commitment to truth or consistency. It connects these features to familiar psychological mechanisms, including source multiplicity, familiarity, peripheral credibility cues, and first-impression effects.

- Source: [Paul & Matthews, RAND PE-198](https://www.rand.org/pubs/perspectives/PE198.html)
- Use in this skill: count independent evidence origins, not messages, and record contradictions.
- Limitation: the case concerns Russian propaganda; transfer the mechanisms with care rather than treating every repeated claim as coordinated propaganda.

## Reflection helps, but does not abolish bias

Levine’s truth-default theory explains that people generally presume honesty unless suspicion is triggered, with belief congruity, social congruence, and repetition influencing acceptance. Pennycook and Rand found that cognitive reflection correlated with better discernment between real and fake headlines, including ideologically congruent ones.

- Sources: [Levine (2022), PubMed PMID 35763893](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35763893/), [Pennycook & Rand (2019), PubMed PMID 29935897](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29935897/)
- Use in this skill: make a deliberate disconfirming question and evidence pause routine.
- Limitation: these studies do not prove that reflection reliably defeats motivated reasoning in every domain or group.

## Prebunk techniques, not only individual claims

Roozenbeek, van der Linden, and Nygren found that a Bad News intervention improved recognition of simulated manipulation techniques across four language versions. The techniques included impersonation, emotional language, polarization, conspiracy, discrediting, and trolling.

- Source: [Roozenbeek et al. (2020), HKS Misinformation Review](https://misinforeview.hks.harvard.edu/article/global-vaccination-badnews/)
- Use in this skill: name the technique, then investigate the concrete claim it carries.
- Limitation: voluntary, self-selected pre-post samples and uncertain long-term durability; prebunking complements verification.

## Do not infer lying from demeanor

An open-access review concludes that no non-verbal sign has been shown to reliably identify lying at the individual level; it describes a meta-analysis of more than 25,000 veracity judgments with average accuracy near chance.

- Source: [Denault et al. (2020), *Research on Non-verbal Signs of Lies and Deceit: A Blind Alley*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7767987/)
- Use in this skill: evaluate content, provenance, context, and corroboration rather than confidence, eye contact, voice, or body language.
- Limitation: this does not imply every speaker is equally credible; it rejects demeanor as a dependable individual lie detector.
